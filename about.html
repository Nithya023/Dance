Deep Dive into India's Classical Dances

Explore the rich history, intricate movements, and cultural significance of India's most revered traditional dance forms.

Bharatanatyam

History and Origin

Bharatanatyam, originating from the temples of Tamil Nadu in South India, is one of the oldest and most revered classical dance forms in India. Its roots can be traced back to the ancient Hindu scripture, the Natya Shastra, a treatise on performing arts. Traditionally performed by Devadasis (temple dancers) in temples and royal courts, Bharatanatyam is a solo dance.

Key Characteristics

This dance combines three main elements: Nritta (pure dance movements), Nritya (expressive dance with gestures and facial expressions), and Natya (drama or storytelling). It is characterized by its intricate footwork, graceful body movements, and elaborate hand gestures known as mudras, which convey a wide range of emotions and narratives. The expressive abhinaya, or facial expressions, are central to conveying the emotional depth of the stories.

Costumes, Music, and Themes

Dancers wear vibrant silk sarees, often adorned with traditional gold jewelry, and their faces are meticulously made up to highlight expressions. The music accompanying Bharatanatyam is Carnatic, featuring instruments like the mridangam (drum), violin, flute, and nattuvangam (cymbals). The thematic content predominantly revolves around Hindu mythological stories, devotional hymns, and philosophical concepts, often portraying the love and devotion between deities.

The dance begins with an Alarippu, a pure dance piece, and progresses through various stages like Jatiswaram, Shabdam, Varnam (the central and most elaborate piece), Padam, and Tillana, concluding with a Mangalam. Bharatanatyam is not merely a performance; it is a spiritual practice, a form of yoga, and a profound medium for storytelling and devotion. Its revival in the 20th century, particularly by Rukmini Devi Arundale, brought it from temple precincts to global stages, making it a symbol of Indian cultural heritage. The rigorous training involved in Bharatanatyam instills discipline, grace, and a deep understanding of rhythm and expression.

Kathak

History and Origin

Kathak, primarily originating from Uttar Pradesh in North India, is a classical dance form known for its storytelling tradition. The word "Kathak" is derived from "Katha," meaning story, and "Kathakar," meaning storyteller. Historically, Kathakars would use music, dance, and gestures to narrate tales from Hindu scriptures, particularly those of Radha and Krishna.

Evolution and Style

Over centuries, Kathak evolved significantly under the influence of both Hindu and Mughal cultures. During the Mughal era, it moved from temples to royal courts, where it acquired elements of Persian and Central Asian dance, leading to a greater emphasis on abstract movements, intricate footwork, and dazzling pirouettes (chakkars). Kathak is unique among Indian classical dances for its blend of Hindu devotional themes and Islamic courtly aesthetics.

Key Elements and Gharanas

The dance is characterized by fast, rhythmic footwork (tatkar) performed with ankle bells (ghungroos), intricate hand movements, and expressive facial gestures. Dancers often engage in a dialogue with the tabla (drum) player, improvising complex rhythmic patterns. There are three main gharanas (schools) of Kathak: Jaipur, Lucknow, and Benares, each with its distinct style and emphasis. The Jaipur gharana is known for its intricate footwork and pure dance, the Lucknow gharana for its grace and abhinaya (expression), and the Benares gharana for its emphasis on spiritual and devotional aspects.

Costumes vary, with Hindu dancers often wearing lehenga-choli or saree, and Muslim dancers opting for anarkali suits or churidar-kurta, reflecting the dual heritage. Kathak performances typically include pure dance (Nritta) sections like Thaat, Amad, Paran, and Toda, and expressive dance (Nritya) sections that involve storytelling through mime and gesture. The dance form demands immense control over rhythm, balance, and expression, allowing the dancer to portray a wide range of emotions and characters. Kathak remains a vibrant and dynamic art form, continually evolving while preserving its rich historical and cultural roots.

Odissi

History and Origin

Odissi, originating from the temples of Odisha (formerly Orissa) in Eastern India, is one of the oldest surviving classical dance forms, with its history dating back over 2,000 years. It was primarily performed by 'Maharis' (temple dancers) and 'Gotipuas' (young boys dressed as girls) to appease the deities, particularly Lord Jagannath.

Characteristics and Poses

Odissi is renowned for its lyrical beauty, fluid movements, and sculpturesque poses, often resembling the ancient temple carvings of Odisha. The dance is characterized by the Tribhanga (triple bend) posture, where the body is gracefully curved at the neck, torso, and knee, and the Chauka, a square-like stance symbolizing Lord Jagannath. These two basic positions form the foundation of Odissi vocabulary. The movements are soft, undulating, and sensual, emphasizing grace and devotion rather than sharp, angular lines.

Thematic Content and Performance

The thematic content of Odissi is predominantly devotional, revolving around the worship of Lord Jagannath and the divine love stories of Radha and Krishna, particularly from Jayadeva's Gita Govinda. The costumes are vibrant and elaborate, typically a specially stitched silk saree, adorned with intricate silver jewelry unique to Odisha, and a distinctive hairstyle. The music is Odissi music, a classical form distinct from Carnatic or Hindustani, featuring instruments like the pakhawaj, flute, sitar, and violin.

An Odissi performance typically begins with Mangalacharan (an invocation), followed by Batunritya (pure dance), Pallavi (elaboration of a musical theme), Abhinaya (expressive dance), and concludes with Moksha (salvation), a fast-paced pure dance that symbolizes spiritual liberation. The dance emphasizes 'Bhava' (mood or emotion) and 'Rasa' (aesthetic flavor), aiming to transport the audience into a spiritual experience. Odissi's revival in the mid-20th century, led by gurus like Kelucharan Mohapatra, brought it to national and international prominence, preserving its ancient traditions while allowing for contemporary interpretations. It is a dance of devotion, poetry, and profound spiritual expression.

Kathakali

History and Origin

Kathakali, a spectacular dance-drama form, originates from Kerala in South India. Evolving in the 17th century, it is a unique blend of drama, dance, music, and ritual, primarily depicting stories from Hindu epics like the Ramayana, Mahabharata, and ancient Puranas.

Distinctive Makeup and Costumes

What sets Kathakali apart is its elaborate and vibrant makeup, intricate costumes, and detailed facial masks, which transform performers into mythical characters, deities, demons, and heroes. The makeup, applied over several hours, is categorized into various 'veshams' or types, such as Pacha (green, for noble heroes), Kathi (knife, for anti-heroes with streaks of evil), Kari (black, for forest dwellers and hunters), Thaadi (beard, with red for evil, white for divine, and black for wild characters), and Minukku (radiant, for female characters and sages). Each color and pattern has symbolic meaning.

Expressions and Performance

Gestures (mudras) are highly codified and extensive, allowing performers to convey complex narratives and emotions without uttering a single word. The eyes play a crucial role, with intricate eye movements (netrabhinaya) expressing a vast spectrum of feelings. The accompanying music, known as 'Sopana Sangeetham,' is unique to Kerala and features instruments like chenda, maddalam, and ilathalam. The vocalists narrate the story, while the dancers interpret it through their movements and expressions.

Training in Kathakali is rigorous and spans many years, involving intense physical exercises, eye exercises, and mastering the vast repertoire of mudras and facial expressions. A performance is a grand spectacle, often lasting several hours, traditionally performed outdoors at night, illuminated by a large brass lamp. Kathakali is not just a dance; it is a profound theatrical experience that immerses the audience in ancient tales, moral dilemmas, and the grandeur of Indian mythology. Its unique visual language and powerful storytelling make it a globally recognized art form.

Manipuri

History and Origin

Manipuri, a classical dance form from the northeastern state of Manipur, stands out for its gentle, fluid, and graceful movements, starkly contrasting with the vigorous and rhythmic styles of other Indian classical dances. Its origins are deeply rooted in the Vaishnavite tradition, particularly the devotion to Radha and Krishna.

Movement Style and Themes

The most prominent theme in Manipuri dance is the Raas Leela, which depicts the divine love play between Radha, Krishna, and the Gopis (cowherd maidens). Unlike other classical forms that emphasize sharp lines and percussive footwork, Manipuri focuses on soft, undulating body movements, circular patterns, and a serene, devotional expression. The dancers' feet are often kept flat on the ground, and ankle bells (ghungroos) are rarely used or are very subtle, contributing to the ethereal and gliding quality of the movements.

Costumes, Music, and Spirituality

The costumes are elaborate and unique: female dancers wear a beautifully embroidered, stiff skirt called a 'Kumil' that extends outwards, along with a translucent veil and intricate jewelry. Male dancers typically wear a dhoti. The music accompanying Manipuri dance is devotional, often featuring the Pung (Manipuri drum), cymbals (kartal), harmonium, and flute, with chanting and singing of devotional poetry.

The performance unfolds with a meditative quality, emphasizing 'bhakti' (devotion) and 'rasa' (aesthetic delight). Manipuri dance is not just a performance but a spiritual offering, aiming to evoke a sense of peace and transcendence in both performers and audience. There are various forms of Manipuri dance, including the more vigorous 'Cholom' performed by male dancers, and the delicate 'Raas' performed by females. The training involves a deep understanding of the Vaishnavite philosophy and a mastery of subtle expressions and body control. Manipuri dance is a beautiful representation of devotion, grace, and the unique cultural heritage of Manipur, captivating audiences with its serene beauty and spiritual depth.

Kuchipudi

History and Origin

Kuchipudi, a classical dance form from the village of Kuchipudi in Andhra Pradesh, South India, is a vibrant blend of dance, drama, and music. Its origins trace back to the 17th century, evolving from a tradition of male Brahmin dancers who performed in temples and villages.

Unique Elements and Characteristics

Unlike other classical forms that are purely dance, Kuchipudi incorporates elements of theatre, including spoken dialogue, singing by the dancer, and elaborate stage settings. The dance is characterized by its quick, intricate footwork, dramatic expressions, and graceful body movements. A unique and famous element of Kuchipudi is the 'Tarangam,' where the dancer performs on a brass plate, moving it with their feet, often while balancing a pot of water on their head or holding a lamp in their hand. This showcases the dancer's exceptional balance and control.

Thematic Content and Performance

The thematic content of Kuchipudi primarily revolves around Hindu mythological stories, particularly those related to Krishna and Vaishnavism. The performances are often accompanied by Carnatic music, with instruments like the mridangam, violin, flute, and tanpura. Dancers wear bright, colorful sarees with traditional jewelry, and their makeup is designed to enhance their expressions.

Kuchipudi emphasizes both 'Nritta' (pure dance) and 'Nritya' (expressive dance), with a strong focus on 'abhinaya' (facial expressions) to convey the emotional depth of the narratives. Each performance typically begins with an invocation, followed by a 'Pravesa Daruvu,' a short dance piece introducing the character. The dance then unfolds through various segments, including pure dance sequences, dramatic enactments, and lyrical interpretations. Kuchipudi training is rigorous, encompassing not only dance techniques but also vocal music, acting, and Sanskrit. The dance form has evolved to include female dancers, and its dynamic nature, expressive storytelling, and challenging technical elements make it a captivating and engaging art form that continues to thrive and gain international recognition.
